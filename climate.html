Definition

Climate Tech or Clean Tech (used more often) is any product or service that aims to reduce negative environmental impact. This can be through inventing and implementing new materials/processes, using sustainable alternatives, increasing efficiency of current systems or educating people. All-in-all anything that keeps ice from melting, sea-levels from rising, heat waves from becoming common etc. (more visible changes, but definitely not limited to these).

Introduction

Two Scenarios.

Scenario 1: It's 2050 - our cars still run on fossil fuel, large factories are still powered by coal. We have failed to use renewable tech at scale. Miami and New Orleans are under water and NYC is the new Bahrain of heat waves. Climate change is our biggest fight.

Scenario 2: It's 2050 - we realized a few things in 2020 and have taken action. Renewable energy is the only source we're exploiting, cars are all-electric. Water in Delhi is as clean as that in Switzerland. But, climate change is still our biggest fight.

In both the scenarios, there are solar farms that existed in 2020 and new ideas to battle the climate change were being generated every now and then. So why the huge difference? Venture Capital has helped finance the the world we are living in right now and it is time that a majority of those investments are directed towards Clean Tech.

The Market

VC investments in Clean Tech and the returns have not been consistent. The market has changed considerably over the past two decades.

Here I have picked up two timeframes to analyze this: 2005-2011 saw a VC boom but most of these investments were not very successful. I have tried to break that down to understand what we can learn from it which can be applied to crack the current Clean Tech market.

2005 - 2011 Boom and Bust

Source: MIT Energy Initiative - Venture Capital and Clean-tech

From 2005 to 2011, VC investors had plowed $25 billion into the clean-tech sector and lost over half of it. In 2008, VC clean-tech investments exceeded $5 billion. By 2013 funding had dropped to $2 billion. As a result of the lack of capital, only 24 clean-tech companies were founded in 2013, compared with 75 companies in 2007.

The VC model did not seem suitable for the clean-tech innovation. Why so?

  • Clean-tech startups required significant capital early
  • Had long development timelines
  • Were uncompetitive in commodity market
  • Could not attract corporate acquirers

VCs look to invest in startups that quickly achieve scale to address a high-growth market and provide large payoffs within a short time frame. Some clean-tech startups during this time did fit in this model - Nest and Opower were both founded during this time and were later acquired by Google and Oracle for $3.2B and $532M respectively. Many other smaller startups with similar sub-sectors and target audiences did well too.

Market Today : Finding the right Clean Tech Opportunity

In 2013, S&P Index along with Russell 3000 gained 31%. Sounds like an exceptional result? Well, it might be still be a bit shy of how clean-tech companies have performed. Income and appreciation combined, clean-tech companies had a 40% return totaling up to $946 Billion in 2019 - more than 3 times what it was in 2010. By contrast, revenues for the 28 companies in the S&P Energy Index, a mostly fossil-fuel crowd, declined 5% in 2019. Falling costs and a decreasing perception of risk have significantly enabled renewable energy implementation over the past decade, and led to expanding mainstream capital investing in the space.
